GENERAL INFORMATION

 

Considerations for all exams:

Universal Precautions:
  • Commonly called Standard Precautions
  • Established by the Center for Disease Control to prevent infection and confine germs
  • Protects the patient AND the caregiver
  • Includes hand washing, equipment cleaning and protective equipment
  • Linens and pillowcases should be changed after each patient
  • Wearing a mask
    • Do not touch the part of the mask that will touch your face
    • Tie the upper strings over the ears and towards the top of the back of your head
    • When finished, remove the gloves first, and then untie the bottom strings, followed by the top strings
    • Discard the mask properly and wash your hands
  • Gowns are worn if soiling of clothing is likely from blood or body fluids
    • Wear gowns that are long and large to cover clothing
    • Wash your hands and put the mask on before putting on a gown
    • Remove all watches and jewelry before putting on a gown
Hand Washing:
  • Wash hands using antibacterial soap with extra care to nails, knuckles and between fingers
  • Say your ABCs or sing Happy Birthday to assist in washing for the proper length of time (at least 15 seconds)
  • Washing should be performed for all of the following situations:
    • Contamination - Immediately wash the affected area with soap and water, notify your supervisor and immediately report to occupational health to file an exposure report
    • After gloves removed
    • Between patient contact
    • After changing exam table linens or paper
Personal Hygiene:
  • Jewelry should be removed while working
  • Porcelain or artificial nails are not permitted
  • Masks are designed for use with a single patient and should not be worn all day
  • If you are ill, you should not come to work
Patient Infection Control and Isolation:
  • Standard precautions should also be applied in all of the situations below
  • Always disinfect the equipment and wash your hands before and after leaving the isolation room
1. Respiratory Isolation
  • AKA droplet precautions
  • Used to protect others from germs in the patient's nose, mouth, throat, and lungs
  • It is used for diseases that are airborne
  • Mask required for staff; some types of pneumonia, mumps, rubella
2. Reverse Protective Isolation
  • AKA protective environment precautions
  • Protects patients from the germs of others
  • It is used to protect patients with a history of chemotherapy, neutropenia, recent organ transplant or immunodeficiency (AIDS, leukemia)
  • Masks for those with cough or cold, no flowers or plants
3. Strict Isolation
  • AKA airborne precautions
  • Separation from others to prevent the spread, by contact or airborne transmission, of highly contagious diseases
  • Requires use of personal respirator for staff/visitors
  • TB, Varicella, Shingles
4. Enteric Precautions
  • Used in diseases involving ingestion of disease-producing microorganisms to protect others from germs in the patient's bowels, bladder, and stomach
  • Gloves and gown required
  • Certain GI and enteric infections
5. Wound-Skin Precautions
  • AKA contact precautions
  • Used to protect others from germs in patient's wounds or any other heavily contaminated areas
  • Require gown and gloves for any type of patient contact
  • All protective equipment should be disposed of in the marked container before leaving the patient room
  • MRSA, VRE, Scabies, Impetigo

Transducer Disinfection:
  • Medical devices are categorized for disinfection based on their use
    • Non-Critical devices come in contact with intact skin (standard fetal echo probes)
    • Semi-Critical devices come in contact with high risk patients, non-intact skin or mucous membranes (endovaginal, endorectal and transesophageal probes)
    • Critical devices are used in a sterile field or within blood vessel (intraoperative or intravascular transducers)
  • Low or Intermediate disinfection (EX: Saniwipes or T-spray) performed for transducers used for standard exams over intact skin
  • High Level Disinfection refers to the use of a chemical solution on a transducer to reduce or eliminate all microorganisms, except bacterial spores
  • A high level disinfectant (HLD) refers to the chemical used to remove all viable microorganisms from a surface
    • The factors to consider when choosing a high level disinfectant are transducer compatibility, ventilation requirements, disinfection time, rinsing requirements, temperature, re-use period, cycle cost, neutralization requirements
    • Examples include Metricide, Cidex, Glutaraldehyde, Ortho-phathaldehyde (OPA), Hydrogen peroxide
    • Always wear the recommended personal protective equipment when disinfecting the transducer
    • Clean debris from the transducer with a towel and mild soap, test the temperature of the HLD, soak, rinse and place in storage
  • Certain chemicals are unsafe to use on the transducer due to the potential for deterioration of the matching layer and glues used to assemble the transducer
  • Never immerse the electrical component of the transducer in the disinfectant
  • Sprays and wipes are NOT acceptable for high level disinfection
  • Sterilization refers to the complete destruction of all living organisms on an object using exposure to heat, chemicals, or radiation
    • Needle guides on transducers are CRITICAL devices that require sterilization after each patient
  • Transducers cannot be thermally sterilized because heating the PE element above the Curie point can cause it to lose its piezoelectric properties; PZT has a Curie point of 300 - 400 degrees Celsius
Sonographer Responsibilities:
  • Always verify you are scanning the correct patient
    • Outpatients - use birth date
    • Inpatients - use hospital wristband and patient chart
  • Confirm the script matches the exam that is scheduled
  • Review any prior pertinent diagnostic exams and reports
  • Take a detailed patient history
  • Explain the exam to the patient and discuss the time frame for results
  • Present pertinent history and prior reports/cases to radiologist
  • Accurately record measurements and comments on tech worksheet
  • Indicate any necessary information regarding patient follow up
  • Disinfect the transducer and machine with a Saniwipe or other approved disinfectant after each patient
  • Gel bottles should be sanitized daily using disinfectant cleanser

Sonographer Communication:

1. ALWAYS verify that you have the proper patient for the requested exam

    • Ask the patient to confirm their name and date of birth prior to starting the exam
    • When performing exams on inpatients, use the patient wrist band to verify the patient name and medical record number
    • If a patient is unconscious and without a wristband, you must confirm the patient identity by contacting their nurse
    • If a patient cannot be verified, do NOT perform the exam and reschedule the exam until verification can be accomplished

2. Confirm script matches exam scheduled

    • When the suspected diagnosis is not able to be evaluated by the exam that is ordered, contact the referring physician to clarify the order
    • The exam can always be rescheduled but it is not the Sonographers role to CANCEL an exam

3. Explain the exam or procedure and answer questions related to how the exam is performed

    • Always consider the patient's mental state, age and other factors that may affect their ability to understand the exam explanation
    • A patient with dementia or Alzheimer's disease may be more comfortable with a family member or something familiar in their room; check with the patient's nurse for tips on communicating with the patient
    • A patient that appears unconscious should still be offered a short explanation of the exam, especially when they have family members in the room
    • Avoid compromising the exam quality and scanning environment whenever possible

4. Obtain pertinent history related to indication for current exam

5. Discuss when the patient can expect the results and that as a Sonographer you cannot provide any results for the exam; tell the patient they need to contact the physician that ordered the exam to obtain the results

6. Ask the patient if they have additional testing scheduled after this exam; offer to assist them with getting to their next exam

7. Accurately record measurements and comments on tech worksheet

8. Indicate any necessary info regarding patient follow up appointment date

****** NEVER provide clinical findings directly to the patient, this information must come from the physician

Emergency Situations:
  • The role of fetal sonographer is not to inform the patient of any findings on the exam
  • Fetal hydrops and fetal demise are examples of critical findings on a fetal echo and you should find a fetal cardiologist as soon as possible
  • A nuchal cord and diaphragmatic hernia are important delivery considerations
CPR on a Pregnant Patient:
  • After 20 weeks gestational age, the pregnant uterus can press against the inferior vena cava and the aorta which limits venous return and cardiac output
  • Extrinsic compression of the IVC and venous return can produce hypotension or shock and in the critically ill patient may precipitate cardiac arrest
  • The uterine size causes a compromise in venous return and cardiac output, which limits the effectiveness of chest compressions
  • The gravid uterus may be shifted away from the inferior vena cava and the aorta by placing the patient 15° to 30° left oblique position or by manually shifting the uterus to the left side
  • CPR
    • 1. Call for help
    • 2. Give 2 compressions
    • 3. Check and position airway
    • 4. Give 2 rescue breaths
  • Fetal monitoring is not important during maternal CPR
  • Try to save the mother first, then worry about the baby
  • The American Heart Association recommends 30 compressions for every 2 rescue breaths
  • The guidelines also state that you start single rescuer CPR with 30 compressions, then reassess patient
  • An overabundance of magnesium sulfate can cause oliguria and cardiac arrest
  • This medication is used to prevent pre-term labor in patients with preeclampsia
Heimlich Maneuver:
  • Choking rescue technique is used to clear an airway obstruction caused by a foreign object in adults and children
  • A sonographer should inform the patient that she/he will try to dislodge the foreign body
  • The sonographer will position themself behind the patient and wrap their arms around the patient in the subxiphoid area
  • Do NOT place hands on the belly over the uterus
  • Care must be taken not to break the xiphoid process so the hands should be placed slightly higher on the breast bone just above where the lowest ribs join; chest compressions, not abdominal thrusts
Pre-term Labor:
  • Low amniotic fluid
  • Frequent contractions
  • The physician should be alerted immediately and the patient should be assessed
  • The nurse or physician should apply the fetal monitor and assess the patient
  • Magnesium sulfate is commonly used to treat preeclampsia and prevent pre-term labor and will most likely be administered to the patient before the fetal echo is resumed
  • Indomethacin can also be used to stop early labor but offers an increased risk of early closure of the ductus arteriosus
